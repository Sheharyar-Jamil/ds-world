FAQ

WHAT IS PERM?

Federal law permits U.S. businesses to hire foreign workers to work in full-time, permanent positions that cannot be adequately filled by the existing U.S. workforce. “PERM” is an acronym for the Department of Labor's (“DOL”) online filing system for this type of application and, colloquially, it is used to describe the entire process.

WHAT ARE THE PROCESSES INVOLVED?

For more details, visit our [Labor Certification page]. If you'd like further information, we can provide resources like a color-coded process chart or a comprehensive 30-page PERM training manual.

HOW MUCH DOES IT COST?

Employers are responsible for costs related to the Labor Certification process, including newspaper ads ($200–$2,000) and legal fees. This process is most cost-effective for employers hiring 10+ workers for the same role. Immigration-related costs, such as visa processing fees, can often be covered by the workers.

HOW LONG DOES IT TAKE?

The process typically takes about a year, with no shortcuts due to sequential steps and increased government processing times. It’s best suited for employers facing ongoing staffing challenges.

HOW MANY PEOPLE CAN I HIRE BASED ON MY RECRUITMENT?

There’s no fixed limit on hires per recruitment round, but the employer must prove it can pay all employees. We’ll work with employers to determine their hiring capacity.

WHO PICKS/HIRES THE OVERSEAS CANDIDATES?

While DS World Consulting can connect you with candidates and assist with interviews, the final hiring decisions are entirely up to the employer.

HOW DO I KNOW THE CANDIDATES CAN DO THE JOB?

Employers can set job parameters, such as shift flexibility, physical abilities, or language skills, for overseas candidates, just as they would for U.S. staff. We help match candidates to roles based on their skills and preferences, ensuring a good fit, with employers having the final say in interviews.

HOW MUCH DO I HAVE TO PAY THE OVERSEAS CANDIDATES?

Employers must pay overseas workers the higher of the Prevailing Wage Determination (PWD) or the employer’s customary wage for similar positions. The PWD is determined by the DOL based on job duties and location. For example, if the PWD is $11.55 but the employer pays $12.00 per hour for similar roles, the foreign worker must be paid $12.00 per hour.

IF I CANNOT SAVE MONEY ON WAGES AND I HAVE TO COVER OTHER COSTS INVOLVED WITH OBTAINING THE LC, HOW DOES THIS MAKE SENSE FOR MY BUSINESS?

This program is ideal for employers facing chronic labor shortages or high turnover, especially for high- or low-skill roles. If your business can retain staff easily, this program may not be necessary. Common roles filled include food production, janitorial, home health aides, and hospitality positions.

HOW LONG WILL THE WORKERS STAY WITH THE EMPLOYER?

Positions must be permanent, full-time, with no set end date. As long as business conditions allow, both employer and employee must agree in good faith. The DOL typically accepts the arrangement if the employee stays with the company for at least one year.

WHERE DO THE WORKERS COME FROM?

Employers can file for Labor Certification anywhere, but workers from high-quality-of-life countries are less interested in relocating. Most candidates come from countries with emerging economies, such as the Philippines, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka. Countries like China and India have long visa backlogs, which makes them less appealing for employers due to the extended waiting times.

I AM LOOKING AT THESE PEOPLE'S RESUMES AND THEY SEEM A LITTLE DIFFERENT THAN I'M USED TO SEEING FOR THIS TYPE OF ROLE. HOW COME? WHY ARE THEY INTERESTED IN THIS JOB?

Prospective employees seek better quality of life in the U.S., including improved education, healthcare, and long-term stability. This desire for a permanent stay benefits employers, as workers are committed to maintaining their jobs. Additionally, many candidates are currently working on temporary visas in the Gulf Region, where uncertainty and stricter immigration policies create a strong interest in long-term U.S. opportunities, making the PERM/EB-3 process a win-win for both workers and employers.

HOW DO I DO A BACKGROUND CHECK ON THE EMPLOYEES IF THEY HAVE NO REAL HISTORY IN THE U.S.?

The U.S. Department of State conducts thorough background checks on workers and their families, verifying their history through international databases and local police clearances. This ensures that all candidates meet U.S. immigration standards, giving employers confidence in their suitability for employment.

I KEEP SEEING THE WORD “SPONSOR” ON VARIOUS DOCUMENTS, WHAT DOES IT MEAN IN THIS CONTEXT?

In the PERM process, terms like “audit” and “sponsor” have specific legal meanings. A “sponsor” refers to the entity (either an employer or a family member) that helps an individual immigrate to the U.S. In the employment context, the sponsor is responsible for paying the worker according to the terms outlined in the PERM application. There are no additional financial requirements like covering travel or housing costs.

I CURRENTLY HAVE WORKERS WITH NON-PERMANENT STATUSES IN THE U.S., CAN WE FILE LC APPLICATIONS FOR THEM?

Employers may have employees in Temporary Protected Status (TPS) or seeking asylum, who have valid work authorization but lack a long-term pathway to stay in the U.S. In many cases, employers can file for a Labor Certification (LC) on their behalf while the application is pending. However, it’s important to carefully review the employee's status, especially with TPS holders, as there may be additional complications or legal restrictions. Please contact us for a thorough review if this applies to your situation.

THIS SEEMS TOO GOOD TO BE TRUE, WHAT COULD GO WRONG?

We compare the PERM process to driving a car: if you follow the rules, there’s minimal risk. We’ll ensure full compliance with the law, but there are potential issues to be aware of. For example, if the DOL finds the domestic recruitment process was not handled properly, they may require "supervised recruitment," where another round of ads is conducted. The DOL may also conduct a site visit to verify workers are performing the job as stated. In extreme cases of fraud, penalties could include temporary bans from the PERM program or even criminal charges. However, with diligent planning and attention, these issues are avoidable, and we will work closely with you to prevent them.
